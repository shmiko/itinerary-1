Historical and Active Santiago

Santiago

 by Eileen Smith
$$
cultural
sightseeing

Whether you've come to Chile for hiking in the glittering lakes region, or to hike into the stiff winds of Patagonia, or even up to the southern outpost of San Pedro de Atacama, land of the moon valley and El Tatio geysers, here's an itinerary to keep you busy, active and educated for a good day and a half in Santiago. Take a local's advice and work up an appetite to appreciate some good eats along the way.

6:00pm

Arrive in Santiago, and take the ultramodern metro to the entrance of Cerro Santa Lucía, at the corner of José Miguel de La Barra and Merced, in the quirky Bellas Artes neighborhood, full of cafés and independently-owned boutiques. This hill, on which Pedro de Valdivia founded the city, houses lots of cute corners and plazas, including one with Moorish tiled benches, perfect for an afternoon rest. Walk up to the top, and regard the city below. You can get a good overview of downtown, and depending on where you’re staying, may even be able to make out your hotel. Don’t miss the “front side” of the hill, with its yellow palace and fountain out front, an excellent photo op at any time of day. You finish on the Alameda (the main street in Santiago), near the Santa Lucía metro.

From here, double back to the corner where you started, flanking the hill and pop into Ópera (Jose Miguel de la Barra esq. Merced, Santiago) for a meal made of typical Chilean ingredients, with an emphasis on fish. The meal starts with an amuse bouche, and the service is attentive. The downstairs restaurant is for more formal dining, or you might choose the relaxed upstairs restaurant and bar Catedrál, which has great live music many nights starting at around 10:00 PM. Try the seafood empanadas with pebre (a fresh salsa).

Saturday

Parque Metropolitano de Santiago
thumbnails
calendar

9:00am

On Saturday morning, head into bohemian Bellavista, with its graffitied alleyways and many sidewalk bars, all of which is nearly silent on a Saturday morning to the very end of Pio Nono, to the entrance of San Cristobal Hill. You’ll see cyclists heading up the hill, and you should follow them, about 1 km uphill until the second small dirt turnout on the right, which is a path called “Zorro Vidal.” This is an easy 40-minute hike at a comfortable pace, with a couple of benches to stop at on the way. You come up through a garden to a small plaza (where there is a water spigot to fill water bottles) called Plaza México, but most people continue on to the busier Plaza Tupahue, to enjoy the breeze that comes up from the city. If you’re lucky, you can see hawks coasting on the thermals. This spot up at the top is the crown of the hill that overlooks the city, and attracts devout Catholics (to visit the Virgin which is just a little bit further up), as well as cyclists, runners, the occasional rollerblader, and lots of families.

Cerro San Cristobal

calendar

11:30am

Take a long, slow amble back down the hill (about 5 km), checking out the elaborate landscaping, fruit trees and other plants. Keep to the right to give cyclists the right of way.

Parque de las Esculturas
thumbnails
calendar

12:30pm

When you get to the bottom of the hill, continue on through the leafy, posh neighborhood of Pedro de Valdivia Norte and on the left, right before crossing the river, you’ll get to the Parque de las Esculturas, a free sculpture garden (Santa Maria 2201). Here you can walk among, pose with, and have long talks near a variety of different modern sculpture. Look up to the east and you’ll see the Andes.

Liguria
thumbnails
calendar

1:30pm

Continue walking along Pedro de Valdivia until you get to your busy lunch stop, Liguria (Providencia 1373). This is a Chilean classic, with slightly upscale versions of Chilean favorites, including some massive sandwiches and spicy crab claws. The sopaipillas (round fry-bread) to start are a classic.

Plaza Brasil
thumbnails
calendar

3:00pm

Take a taxi down to artsy Plaza Brasil (or take the metro from Pedro de Valdivia to Los Héroes, and walk a few blocks north down Avenida Brasil), and check out the fantastical climbing structures at the park, which are part dinosaur, part fantasy. On weekend afternoon, this plaza fills with families, cotton candy vendors and the occasional chess or ping pong game. It’s a great place to observe street live in Santiago. Continue down Huérfanos passing classic early 1900s architecture interspersed with 60s concrete block construction until you reach the street Matucana, where the Museo de La Memoria is.

Museo de la Memoria y los Derechos Humanos
thumbnails
calendar

3:30pm

Museo de la Memoria (Matucama 501) is a multimedia musuem about the Chilean dictatorship, inagurated in 2010, and which gives a historical background, as well as personal stories of resistance in Chile and abroad. Admission is free. The first couple of floors can be jarring, but the exhibits are arranged in such a way that you’re led through to the return of democracy and the great joy that surrounded it, and though it seems impossible, you can actually leave the museum feeling fairly positive.

Parque Quinta Normal
thumbnails
calendar

6:00pm

Cross the street to stroll around newly-renovated, shaded Quinta Normal Park, rent a pedal-cart to try your steering skills among the other cyclists and pedal-car rentals or go paddle boating in the small lagoon, were you’ll be admonished not to bumper-boat. Train lovers will not want to miss the railroad museum, with several important examples of steam-powered and other old Chilean trains installed into a small outside exhibit.

Boulevard Lavaud
thumbnails
calendar

8:00pm

Walk back up Compañía de Jesus as night starts to fall, and consider your dining options. For upscale quirk, there is the knick-knack filled, French-inspired Boulevard Lavaud (Compañía de Jesus 2799) a multi-story mansion-turned restaurant with a tasty mixed seafood platter. Or, across the street, for some a more home-grown, downtown vibe, choose the Cervecería Nacional for a brewpub atmosphere with decent pizzas, a fried shrimp sandwich, and happy hour specials of the house beer for about $2 a glass.

Bar Dos Gardenias
thumbnails
calendar

10:00pm

If the long walks and hearty food haven’t worn you out, head up to Bellavista and try either Bar Dos Gardenias (Antonia Lopez de Bello 0104) for live, local music, which often leans towards a new/old folk style called trova, which features male vocals and guitar, or a more sedate evening of sipping drinks at the patio at the Aubrey Hotel (317 Constitucion), one of the nicest boutique hotels in the city, with what many consider to be the best martini.

Sunday

La Bicicleta Verde
thumbnails
calendar

9:30am

On Sunday morning, stretch your legs and head to Bellas Artes where, just across the bridge from the Museo Bellas Artes, La Bicicleta Verde is waiting for you for your morning bike tour of classic Santiago, with visits to markets and the Plaza de Armas, along with discussions and tours of how, when, and why the neighborhood immediately across the river (La Chimba) was settled. Here, you’ll be outfitted with a bright green cruiser bike (see: company name) and helmet, and your capable guide will take you around some of this area’s must sees, along with stops for hearty local snacks and drinks.

La Vega Chica
thumbnails
calendar

12:30pm

From here, if you’ve still got some more time, try heading back to La Vega (one of the markets you’ll go through with the Bicicleta Verde tour) for a full meal. Ask your Bicicleta Verde guides for their recommendations for any budget, from the lower end fried fish and rice to the most expensive, centolla, king crab legs.
